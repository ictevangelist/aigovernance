The landscape: why govern AI, and what applies to you

Before any rule: why you would govern AI at all, what counts as “AI” here, and the law and guidance the policy sits within.

Home › The landscape: why govern AI, and what applies to you

A good AI policy starts with your reasons for using AI in the first place: what it is there to help with in your setting, whether that is teaching, workload or inclusion. Set that out before any rule appears. The governance is there to serve that purpose, not to get in its way.

What it is

The policy statement sets out how your organisation uses AI: by whom, for what, and within what limits, so you get the benefits without putting at risk the safety of children and adults, data security and privacy, fairness, or professional and academic integrity. It sits within your wider digital strategy. You review it on a set cycle, and again whenever the law, the guidance, or your own use of AI changes in any material way.

What counts as AI here. Generative AI tools, and any software feature that creates content, or that makes or informs decisions or predictions about people. That holds whether the feature is a standalone product, built into software you already approve, or reached through a browser. In practice, it means chatbots, image and content generators, and the AI features now appearing inside everyday software. Long-established automation such as spellcheck or predictive text is out of scope, unless it starts doing one of those things.

Why it matters

AI rarely arrives through one obvious route. It turns up inside the platforms you already license, and behind any browser tab a member of staff cares to open. A policy written only around “the AI tool we bought” will miss most of your real exposure. Defining scope by what a tool does, namely whether it generates content or makes or informs decisions about people, is what keeps your policy relevant as products change around you.

If you are a multi-academy trust, adopt one policy centrally and hold school-level detail in local appendices. Separate versions drift apart over time and become very hard to assure.

What you decide here

  • Your reasons for adopting AI: a sentence or two on what it is there to help with in your setting.
  • Your organisation name, review cycle, and who is in scope (usually all staff, governors, trustees, volunteers and contractors).
  • Any platform AI features that are governed by a separate assessment, noted so the boundary is clear.
  • Whether pupils are in scope. They are not, by default, until the optional module’s conditions are met.

Guidance it speaks to

  • Keeping Children Safe in Education 2026 — the statutory safeguarding baseline from 1 September 2026.
  • UK GDPR & Data Protection Act 2018 (as amended by the Data (Use and Access) Act 2025); ICO guidance on AI and the ICO AI & data protection risk toolkit.
  • ICO Children’s Code, wherever children’s data is involved.
  • Equality Act 2010 and, for public bodies, the public sector equality duty.
  • DfE position on generative AI in education and the DfE generative AI product safety standards (January 2026).
  • JCQ guidance on AI use in assessments, where qualifications are involved.
  • the DfE filtering & monitoring, cyber security, and digital leadership & governance standards.
  • Online Safety Act 2023 and the Ofcom Protection of Children code of practice.
  • Prevent duty guidance (2023) and Working Together to Safeguard Children 2026.
  • The financial governance framework that applies to you: the Academy Trust Handbook; the scheme for financing schools and the Schools Financial Value Standard; or a college’s corporation framework.
  • You can see them all, with what each one requires, on the guidance map.
